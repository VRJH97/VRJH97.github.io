Before any drug can be designed, science must understand the disease. This stage happens in universities, research hospitals, and government labs — often without any pharmaceutical company involvement. It is funded by public grants (NIH, ERC, Wellcome Trust). Most drugs trace their origins to discoveries made 15–30 years before they reached patients.

Validation Methods

  • Genetic knockdown/knockout (siRNA, CRISPR)
  • Overexpression studies in cell lines
  • Human genetic evidence (mendelian randomisation)
  • Antibody or small molecule tool compound studies
  • Patient cohort correlation (mutation → phenotype)

Key Criteria for a Good Target

  • Causal role in disease (not just correlation)
  • Druggable: has a binding pocket or active site
  • Selective: not critical in all healthy tissues
  • Measurable: a clear biomarker for modulation
  • Intellectual property space available

Hit-to-Lead
With a validated target, the hunt begins for molecules that bind to it and modulate its activity. High-throughput screening (HTS) can test millions of compounds in weeks. Computational virtual screening can evaluate billions. A "hit" is any compound that shows measurable activity against the target — this is the raw chemical starting material for drug development.

Screening Approaches

  • High-Throughput Screening: 1–10 million compounds in robotic assays
  • Virtual/Computational: docking, ML scoring of 10B+ compounds
  • Fragment-Based: small pieces that partially bind, then grown
  • DNA-Encoded Libraries: billions of compounds with DNA barcodes
  • Natural products: plant, microbial, and marine compound extracts

Hit Criteria

  • Reproducible activity (confirmed in ≥2 assays)
  • Dose-response relationship (IC50 measured)
  • Selectivity: not hitting everything non-specifically
  • Structure-activity relationship (SAR) tractable
  • No obvious toxicity flags ("PAINS" compounds excluded)

ADMET Properties Optimised

  • Absorption: oral bioavailability, membrane permeability
  • Distribution: plasma protein binding, CNS penetration
  • Metabolism: CYP450 stability, metabolite safety
  • Excretion: half-life, renal clearance
  • Toxicity: hERG, genotoxicity, reactive metabolites

Candidate Nomination Criteria

  • Potency: IC50/EC50 typically <100 nM
  • Selectivity: >100× over off-target proteins
  • Oral bioavailability: >20% (if oral route)
  • Metabolic half-life: >1–2 hours in microsomes
  • No genotoxicity, hERG, or CYP3A4 inhibition flags
  • Lipinski's Rule of Five compliance (or justified deviation)

Before any human is dosed, the candidate must pass rigorous in vitro and in vivo safety testing. This is mandated by regulators globally. Efficacy is demonstrated in animal disease models. Toxicology studies identify dose limits and organ toxicities. This phase produces the Investigational New Drug (IND) application — the document that asks regulators permission to test in humans.

Required Safety Studies

  • Genotoxicity: Ames test, micronucleus assay
  • Safety pharmacology: cardiovascular, CNS, respiratory
  • Acute + repeat-dose toxicity (2 species, rodent + non-rodent)
  • Reproductive/developmental toxicity
  • Carcinogenicity (for chronic use drugs)
  • Phototoxicity, local tolerance as applicable

IND Application Contents

  • All preclinical safety and efficacy data
  • Proposed clinical protocol (Phase I design)
  • Investigator qualifications
  • Chemistry, Manufacturing & Controls (CMC)
  • Proposed starting dose in humans (MRSD)

What's Measured

  • Maximum Tolerated Dose (MTD) or Maximum Feasible Dose
  • Dose-Limiting Toxicities (DLTs) at each escalation step
  • PK: Cmax, Tmax, AUC, half-life, clearance, volume of distribution
  • Bioavailability (F%) if oral formulation
  • Food effect, drug-drug interactions with key perpetrators

Regulatory Context

  • Regulated under IND (FDA) or Clinical Trial Authorisation (EMA)
  • Ethics committee (IRB/IEC) approval mandatory
  • ICH E6 Good Clinical Practice guidelines
  • Serious Adverse Events reported to regulator within 7–15 days
  • Results registered and published on ClinicalTrials.gov
⚠ Phase I success has fallen from 75% (2006–08) to 47% today. As drugs become more targeted and complex (biologics, ADCs, gene therapies), predicting human safety from animal models has become harder, not easier.

Phase II Sub-types

  • Phase IIa: Proof-of-concept — does it show any signal?
  • Phase IIb: Dose-ranging — what is the optimal dose?
  • Randomised, placebo-controlled design (gold standard)
  • Adaptive designs: stopping early for futility or success
  • Basket/umbrella trials in oncology (biomarker-stratified)

Why Phase II Fails So Often

  • Target was valid but wrong patient population chosen
  • Dose was too low (toxicity fear from Phase I)
  • Biomarker was wrong — didn't select responders
  • Disease was more complex than model predicted
  • Placebo effect was underestimated

Trial Design Elements

  • Primary endpoint: pre-specified, clinically meaningful outcome
  • Comparator: placebo OR standard-of-care (SoC)
  • Pre-specified statistical analysis plan (SAP)
  • Independent Data Monitoring Committee (IDMC) oversight
  • Interim analyses with stopping rules (futility/efficacy)

Expedited Pathways (FDA)

  • Breakthrough Therapy: compelling early clinical evidence
  • Accelerated Approval: surrogate endpoint (e.g. tumour shrinkage)
  • Priority Review: potentially major therapeutic advance
  • Fast Track: serious condition + unmet need
  • 66% of 2024 FDA novel approvals used ≥1 expedited pathway
⚠ Phase III failures are catastrophic — billions already spent. Recent examples: Alzheimer's (amyloid antibodies), NASH (FXR agonists), heart failure. Each failure shakes industry confidence in entire mechanistic classes.

The New Drug Application (NDA) or Biologics License Application (BLA) compiles every piece of data generated over the prior decade into a submission that can run to millions of pages. FDA review takes 10–12 months (standard) or 6 months (priority). Once approved, the drug label defines who can receive it, at what dose, with what warnings.

Submission Contents (CTD Format)

  • Module 2: Quality, safety, and efficacy summaries
  • Module 3: All CMC (Chemistry, Manufacturing, Controls) data
  • Module 4: All preclinical study reports
  • Module 5: All clinical study reports (every trial)
  • Risk Management Plan (RMP) for post-approval safety

Global Agencies

  • FDA (USA): NDA for small molecules, BLA for biologics
  • EMA (Europe): Marketing Authorisation Application (MAA)
  • PMDA (Japan), NMPA (China), Health Canada
  • WHO Prequalification for global access programmes
  • Median FDA lead over EMA: ~0.1 year (nearly simultaneous)

Approval is not the end. Phase IV studies and pharmacovigilance monitor safety in the real-world population — which is always larger and more diverse than trial populations. Rare adverse events only visible at 1-in-10,000 frequency only emerge here. New indications (label expansions) require their own clinical trials, restarting the pipeline for the same molecule.

Post-Market Activities

  • Spontaneous adverse event reporting (Yellow Card, MedWatch)
  • Periodic Safety Update Reports (PSURs) to regulators
  • Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategies (REMS) compliance
  • Real-World Evidence studies using EHR and claims data
  • Phase IV commitment trials (often mandated by FDA at approval)

The Data Feedback Loop

  • Real-world outcomes feed back into DrugBank, ClinVar
  • New genetic associations of ADRs discovered → PharmGKB
  • Post-market signals trigger label changes or withdrawals
  • Repurposing opportunities identified (e.g. sildenafil → pulmonary hypertension)
  • Data informs the next generation of drug discovery

Why Drugs Fail
40–50%
Lack of Efficacy
The drug simply doesn't work well enough in humans. Animal models failed to predict the human response. This is the dominant failure mode — and the hardest to fix.
20–30%
Safety / Toxicity
Unexpected adverse effects emerge in larger, more diverse patient populations. Often organ-specific (liver, heart, kidney) and not predicted by animal studies.
10–15%
Pharmacokinetics
The drug is metabolised too quickly, doesn't reach the target organ, or has unacceptable drug-drug interactions. Often fixable with formulation but not always.
10–15%
Commercial / Strategic
The drug works but the market doesn't justify continued investment — competitor approved first, target patient population too small, manufacturing costs prohibitive.
5–10%
Formulation / CMC
The active compound can't be made into a stable, manufacturable product at scale. Biologics and peptides are particularly vulnerable here.
5%
Regulatory
Regulatory agencies request additional studies or find deficiencies in the submission that require programme-delaying responses. Relatively rare once Phase III is complete.

The AI Revolution — What's Changing in 2025–26

For the first time in 2025, a drug with both target and molecule designed entirely by AI completed Phase IIa trials, showing dose-dependent improvement in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis patients. It reached preclinical candidate nomination in 18 months — a process that traditionally takes 3–4 years. Over 173 AI-discovered drug programmes are now in clinical development.
